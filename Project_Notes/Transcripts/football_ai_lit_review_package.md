# football_ai_lit_review_package

Football AI Literature Review Package
Verified master table, APA 7-style references, and a ready-to-use literature review draft
Prepared from the uploaded source list and cross-checked against publisher/DOI records

What is in this document
This package includes 37 papers from the uploaded note, with official title normalization where the note used shorthand or a variant title. The evidence table is grouped to support a literature review on football tactical analytics, sports AI, explainability, and coach-facing design. A few country fields are intentionally left blank where author affiliation was not sufficiently clear from the source metadata.

Section 3. Literature review draft
The literature on football tactical analytics has progressed from descriptive movement analysis to increasingly sophisticated AI-driven decision-support systems. Early syntheses established the methodological foundations for tactical analysis using positional data. Memmert, Lemmink, and Sampaio (2017) explained how positional tracking could be used to study space, time, and coordinated movement, while Low et al. (2020) systematized collective tactical behaviour metrics such as synchronization, dispersion, and team coordination. These reviews are important because they show that football tactics can be quantified in a structured and theoretically grounded way, creating the basis for later machine-learning work.
After this methodological phase, research increasingly moved toward model-based tactical interpretation. Andrienko et al. (2021) showed that football tactics can be understood through carefully constructed spatial-temporal representations, and Goes et al. (2021) demonstrated that tracking-derived tactical features can be linked to team performance outcomes. Ötting and Karlis (2023) advanced this line of work with a copula-based hidden Markov model for tactic classification, while Sotudeh (2025) reviewed formation-identification methods in association football. Together, these studies indicate that tactical behaviour is no longer treated only as a descriptive coaching concept; it is increasingly represented as a set of measurable states, structures, and transitions that can be analysed computationally.
A more recent stream of research has focused on direct football decision support. Process mining has been used to identify tactical sequences from event data (Kröckel & Bodendorf, 2020), and computer vision has enabled automatic tactical-statistic generation from video in lower-infrastructure settings (Theagarajan & Bhanu, 2021). Applied predictive models have also broadened the analytical agenda: player- and position-adjusted expected goals (Hewitt & Karakuş, 2023), substitution prediction (Mohandas et al., 2023), tactical adaptation through Markov games (Hirotsu et al., 2024), plus-minus scouting models (Bajons & Hornik, 2025), and player-value forecasting with explainable machine learning (van Arem et al., 2025). This body of work suggests that football AI is shifting from post-hoc analysis toward systems that can support planning, selection, and in-game decisions.
The strongest emblem of this shift is TacticAI (Wang et al., 2024), which shows that AI can be designed as a practical tactical assistant rather than merely a predictive engine. TacticAI is especially important because it frames AI outputs in ways that are intended to support expert decision-makers, which is much closer to real coaching practice. Additional football-specific reviews reinforce this trend. Teixeira et al. (2025) mapped AI methods for tactical behaviour and collective dynamics, Alves et al. (2025) reviewed social-network approaches to football coordination, and Moya et al. (2025) synthesized recent machine-learning work in professional football. Collectively, these studies show a rapidly maturing football analytics literature, but they also reveal that much of the field still prioritizes predictive performance over transparency, coach usability, and accessibility outside elite clubs.
The broader sports-AI literature helps clarify why these issues matter. Reviews across elite sports and sport sciences describe clear opportunities for AI-driven performance analysis, health monitoring, biomechanics, and injury prediction, but they repeatedly point to the same practical constraints: fragmented data, limited standardization, trust concerns, and weak interpretability (Hammes et al., 2022; Muñoz-Macho et al., 2024; Musat et al., 2024; Kumar et al., 2025). Studies on athlete and coach acceptance make this problem even more concrete. Wachholz et al. (2025) found that trust remains a central issue when users evaluate AI-generated exercise plans, and Dindorf et al. (2025) showed that the perceived suitability of AI-driven sports coaches depends strongly on psychological and perceptual factors. Wang (2025) further extends the relevance of digital football tools beyond professional clubs by showing how technology can support football development in non-elite contexts.
This is where explainability and human-centred design become especially important. Kranzinger et al. (2026) provide one of the clearest sport-specific syntheses of explainable AI, while Schwalbe and Finzel (2024) and Yang et al. (2023) offer the broader conceptual language needed to define explainability rigorously. In parallel, Camilleri et al. (2025) illustrate how explainability can be embedded early in the design of multimodal sensing systems, and the HCI-oriented work of Postma et al. (2022) and Smith et al. (2025) shows that sports technology should be evaluated not only by accuracy but also by interaction design, feedback structure, and fit with real training environments. These studies collectively support the argument that a useful football AI system should not only predict well; it should also communicate clearly, align with coaches' workflows, and remain usable in low-resource settings.
Overall, the literature supports a strong research gap at the intersection of football tactical analytics, explainable AI, and coach-friendly system design. There is already substantial evidence that positional data, event data, video analysis, and machine-learning models can reveal meaningful tactical patterns. However, fewer studies translate these capabilities into transparent, accessible tools that help coaches and players understand why a recommendation was made. A study that develops or evaluates an explainable, human-centred football tactical support system would therefore be well justified, particularly if it targets practical use beyond elite, data-rich environments.
